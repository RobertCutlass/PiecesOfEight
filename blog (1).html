Stand in front of a jeweller's window and you are looking at one of economics' favourite puzzles: an object with almost no industrial use, no cash flow, and — contrary to the marketing — no genuine scarcity, yet a price that has held firm for the better part of a century.

Diamonds are not rare. For most of the twentieth century the supply was controlled by a single dominant producer, De Beers, which released stones into the market slowly and bought up surplus to keep prices from falling. The scarcity was managed, not natural — a textbook case of a producer manufacturing a price floor by withholding quantity.

But supply control alone does not explain why people kept paying. The other half of the story is demand, and it too was engineered. The mid-century advertising campaign that fused diamonds with engagement turned a commodity into a social ritual, and built in a powerful taboo against reselling — which keeps the secondary market thin and the official price unchallenged.

That combination is the signature of a Veblen good: an item whose desirability rises with its price, because the price is the point. A gemstone is, in large part, a store of social signalling rather than a store of fundamental value, and signalling is exactly the kind of demand that high prices reinforce instead of choke off.

The interesting question for the coming decade is what laboratory-grown stones do to all of this. They are chemically identical and far cheaper. If the signal can be bought cheaply, the theory says it eventually stops working — so the real test of the diamond's value was never the carbon, but whether the story survives competition.

Britain has been losing pubs at a striking rate since the turn of the century, with thousands shutting their doors. The decline is steep enough that it looks less like ordinary business churn and more like a structural shift worth modelling properly.

The usual explanations come in a familiar list: rising beer duty, the 2007 indoor smoking ban, climbing commercial rents and business rates, the restrictive "beer tie" that bound many tenants to their breweries, and the long fall in the price of supermarket alcohol relative to a pint in the pub.

Put those into a simple panel of closures across regions and years and a handful of variables do a lot of the work — duty and the relative price of off-trade alcohol especially. But a stubborn residual remains: closures that the economic variables alone do not predict.

That residual is probably cultural rather than financial. Drinking has moved toward the home, younger cohorts drink noticeably less, and an evening out now competes with streaming and a full fridge. Those forces are real but hard to price, which is exactly why they hide in the error term.

The policy reading is the uncomfortable one. A pub is partly a public good — the "local" as a place of low-level community — whose social value never shows up in its own accounts. When the private business fails, the public loss is larger than the balance sheet admits, and that gap is the case for intervention.

The carry trade is one of the oldest apparent free lunches in finance: borrow in a currency with low interest rates, lend in one with high rates, and pocket the difference. For long stretches of history it has quietly worked.

In theory it should not. Uncovered interest parity says the high-yield currency ought to depreciate by just enough to erase the interest advantage. That it frequently fails to do so is the famous "forward premium puzzle," and one of the most durable anomalies in international macroeconomics.

The catch lies in the shape of the returns. Carry pays small, steady gains for months or years, then delivers sudden violent losses when global risk appetite turns and everyone unwinds at once. The trade has been aptly described as picking up nickels in front of a steamroller.

After a decade of near-zero rates almost everywhere, the trade thinned out — there was little yield gap left to harvest. Now that rate differentials have reopened, it is back. But so is the crowding problem: everyone watches the same screens and reaches for the same positions, which makes the unwind faster and sharper.

The honest conclusion is that carry still pays, but it pays a risk premium rather than reflecting a mispricing. You are being compensated for holding crash risk — and the moment you forget that is usually the moment the steamroller speeds up.
